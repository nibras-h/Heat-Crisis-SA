What Heat Does to Your Body

🧠
Heat Stroke
Body temperature soars above 40°C. Brain and organs fail. Death can occur within hours. Karachi's 141 deaths on June 25 were mostly heat stroke.
💧
Dehydration Crisis
In extreme heat, bodies lose water faster than they can drink. Many victims in Karachi had no access to clean water due to power outages affecting pumps.
💔
Cardiac Collapse
Heat strains the heart dangerously. Elderly people and those with chronic conditions are most vulnerable. Most Karachi victims were aged 60–70.
💼
Occupational Death
Construction workers, street vendors, and laborers face deadly heat with no rest periods, no wages if they stop, no protection. Karachi's informal workers bore the heaviest burden.
🤰
Maternal Risk
Pregnant women in heat face miscarriage, preterm birth, and low birth weight. Karachi's pregnant residents had no safe cooling spaces in 2024.
🚑
Hospital Breakdown
Civil Hospital Karachi received 267 heatstroke admissions in 4 days alone. Health systems collapse when heat overwhelms them all at once.

Stories from June 2024

These stories represent real experiences documented during the June 2024 crisis.

Meera, Akbar, and Fatima are fictionalized composites based on documented conditions and events. They represent thousands of real people who faced the heat crisis that week.

MEERA
Street Vendor, Karachi (June 2024)
Meera, 34, has sold fruit on the same Karachi street corner for 8 years. In June 2024, when temperatures hit 47.2°C, she was one of thousands who couldn't stop working. No employer, no wages if she rested, no air conditioning at home. By midday, her body was shutting down. "I had to keep selling. My children depend on me. But the heat doesn't care about that." She collapsed and was rushed to Civil Hospital. She survived—but many didn't.
AKBAR
Construction Worker, Karachi (June 2024)
Akbar, 42, worked on a building site in an informal settlement where temperatures reached 50°C due to dense housing and no green space. On June 25, 2024—the deadliest day—Akbar went to work like always. "The contractor said we had to finish the job. There were no breaks in the heat. By afternoon I couldn't think straight." He was one of 2,547 people admitted to Karachi's hospitals that week with heatstroke. Many didn't make it out.
FATIMA
Pregnant Woman, Karachi (June 2024)
Fatima, 28 weeks pregnant, faced 47°C heat in her small informal settlement house with no air conditioning. The power cuts left her unable to turn on a fan. Heat during pregnancy increases risks of miscarriage and preterm birth. Midwives reported that summer: "80% of babies born during heat waves came too early with respiratory problems." Fatima's story ended in heartbreak. But it could have been prevented. It should have been prevented.

Summer 2026 Is Coming. Prevent This From Happening Again.

Two years have passed. The next heatwave could strike in 4 months. Karachi's government has the power to act. What's needed: immediate, coordinated action on four fronts.

Early Warning Systems: Real-time heat alerts (SMS, radio) giving communities 2–3 days advance notice to seek cooling. Saves 30–50% of lives.
Community Cooling Centers: 200 public centers in informal settlements providing free refuge, water, medical support during peak heat hours.
Worker Protection: Government-enforced rest periods for outdoor workers during extreme heat, with wage guarantees. Protects 3+ million informal workers.
Health System Readiness: Hospital heat wards, trained 500+ health workers, rapid ambulance response. Reduces heat stroke fatality from 40–50% to under 10%.
Green Infrastructure: Trees, green roofs, parks in informal settlements. Cools neighborhoods by 2–4°C and improves air quality.

Cost: PKR 3–5 billion (USD 50–80M) over 5 years. Value: 1,500–2,250 lives saved. PKR 175–300 billion (USD 3–5B) in health and productivity savings.
